## Overview

Indigenous and local knowledge systems represent generations of observation, experimentation, and adaptation to specific environments. In pastoral contexts, this includes knowledge of seasonal grazing patterns, water sources, livestock health, weather indicators, and ecological dynamics. This knowledge is not anecdotal — it is systematic, place-based, and often more fine-grained than scientific monitoring.

Global frameworks increasingly recognise the value of indigenous knowledge. IPBES (the Intergovernmental Platform on Biodiversity and Ecosystem Services) explicitly integrates it alongside scientific knowledge. The Paris Agreement calls for indigenous participation in climate decision-making. Yet in practice, indigenous knowledge is still frequently marginalised in policy and development processes.

## Key Claims

- Indigenous peoples steward roughly a quarter of the world's land, containing significant biodiversity and carbon stores
- Traditional practices such as cultural burning, agroforestry, and rotational grazing offer proven climate adaptation and mitigation solutions
- Achieving Paris Agreement goals requires full participation of indigenous peoples in climate decision-making and direct access to climate financing
- Indigenous knowledge is not merely complementary to science — it offers distinct insights that scientific methods alone cannot provide
