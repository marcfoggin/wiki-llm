## Overview

Pastoral livelihoods depend on shared resources — grazing land, water points, migration corridors. These are typically managed through customary commons governance: community-level rules, reciprocal access agreements, and adaptive institutions that have evolved over generations.

The "tragedy of the commons" narrative assumes shared resources are inevitably overexploited. Elinor Ostrom's Nobel Prize-winning work demonstrated the opposite: communities can and do manage commons sustainably when conditions are right. Her eight design principles describe what successful commons governance looks like.

When commons governance is undermined — by land privatisation, state takeover, or exclusion from protected areas — the result is often the very degradation that was predicted, but caused by the policy change itself rather than by communal use.

## Ostrom's Eight Principles for Managing the Commons

1. **Clearly defined boundaries** — specify who can access what resources
2. **Rules fit local circumstances** — no one-size-fits-all; adapt to local ecology
3. **Participatory decision-making** — involve community members in rule creation
4. **Monitoring** — track compliance with established rules
5. **Graduated sanctions** — use warnings and fines rather than immediate bans
6. **Accessible conflict resolution** — provide informal, affordable dispute mediation
7. **Right to organise** — higher authorities must recognise commons rules as legitimate
8. **Nested networks** — connect local management with regional cooperation when needed

## Key Claims

- The "tragedy of the commons" is real but not inevitable — community governance can prevent it
- Privatisation and state control of pastoral lands often cause more degradation than commons governance
- Effective commons management requires local participation, adaptive rules, and recognition by higher authorities
- Commons are not just resources — they are social institutions that sustain relationships and identity
