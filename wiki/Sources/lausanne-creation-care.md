## Summary

The Lausanne Movement argues that creation care is inseparable from the gospel itself and therefore integral to the Great Commission. Environmental stewardship is not peripheral to Christian mission but central to proclaiming Jesus's lordship over all creation. Drawing on *The Cape Town Commitment* (2010), the authors assert: "we cannot separate our relationship to Christ from how we act in relation to the earth."

The theological foundation roots creation care in God's role as Creator, Christ's sustenance and future redemption of creation, and the Holy Spirit's ongoing renewal of the earth. The report frames ecological responsibility as essential to Christian discipleship and authentic witness — not as an optional extra.

The report documents multiple overlapping environmental crises using the "planetary boundaries" framework. It highlights that "biosphere integrity" and "biochemical flows" have crossed into dangerous red zones, with wildlife decimated — only 4% of earth's mammals remain wild. The authors pose a provocative missiological question: proclaiming good news about Jesus while destroying God's creation undermines Christian credibility and raises serious doubts about Christ's actual lordship in believers' lives.

The Lausanne Movement proposes "integral mission" encompassing three interconnected dimensions: spiritual, social, and creational. Rather than viewing creation care as optional or secondary, the report insists all three dimensions must be integrated into comprehensive mission strategy. Churches need "adequate metrics" to measure not only evangelistic reach but also social and ecological impacts of their work.

Three case studies demonstrate creation care in practice: indigenous peoples' relational approach to land and faith; A Rocha Ghana's successful campaign against destructive bauxite mining in the Atewa Forest; and OMF International's integration of environmental stewardship across diverse ministry contexts. These show creation care functioning as both "posture" and practical commitment rather than merely programmatic add-ons.

The authors caution against two pitfalls: losing evangelistic focus through secularised environmentalism, or ignoring biblical mandates for creation and social care. When Christians demonstrate genuine ecological stewardship, "people take the gospel seriously" — environmental responsibility strengthens rather than distracts from evangelistic effectiveness.

## Key Claims

- Creation care is integral to the Great Commission, not a secondary concern
- The church's gospel witness depends on taking creation seriously
- Only 4% of earth's mammals remain wild — biosphere integrity has crossed dangerous thresholds
- Integral mission requires spiritual, social, and creational dimensions
- Churches need metrics for ecological impact alongside evangelistic reach
