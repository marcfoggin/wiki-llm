## Summary

*Laudato Si'* ("Praise Be to You"), subtitled "On Care for Our Common Home," is Pope Francis's 2015 encyclical addressing the environmental crisis as a moral and spiritual issue. It is the most significant papal document on ecology and has had enormous influence across Christian traditions, not only Catholicism.

The encyclical argues that environmental degradation and social injustice are inseparable — they share common roots in a "throwaway culture" driven by consumerism, short-term thinking, and a distorted understanding of humanity's relationship with creation. Francis introduces the concept of "integral ecology" — the insistence that ecological, economic, social, cultural, and spiritual dimensions of life are all interconnected and cannot be addressed in isolation.

The document draws on scientific evidence (including climate science), Catholic social teaching, the insights of other Christian traditions, and indigenous perspectives. It critiques both technocratic approaches that treat nature as a mere resource and economic models that prioritise profit over people and planet.

Key themes include: the cry of the earth and the cry of the poor are one cry; climate change disproportionately harms the vulnerable; water is a fundamental human right; biodiversity loss impoverishes all of humanity; and genuine progress requires redefining what we mean by development.

The encyclical calls for ecological conversion — a transformation of hearts and lifestyles — alongside systemic changes in politics, economics, and international governance. It has become a reference point for interfaith environmental dialogue and a bridge between secular environmental movements and faith communities.

## Key Claims

- Environmental degradation and social injustice share common roots and must be addressed together
- "Integral ecology" connects ecological, economic, social, cultural, and spiritual dimensions
- The cry of the earth and the cry of the poor are one cry
- Climate change disproportionately harms the most vulnerable
- Genuine progress requires redefining development beyond economic growth
