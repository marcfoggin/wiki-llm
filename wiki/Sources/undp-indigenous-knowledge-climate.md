## Summary

This UNDP article makes the case that indigenous knowledge systems are essential — not merely supplementary — for effective climate action. It documents specific indigenous practices that function as proven climate solutions: the milpa system used by Maya communities in Central America rotates crops like corn, beans, and squash within forested areas, allowing natural forest regeneration and maintaining biodiversity. In West Africa, agroforestry systems in Mali and Burkina Faso combine crops with trees to reduce soil erosion, improve fertility, and protect livelihoods against extreme weather. Aboriginal Australians practice "cultural burning" — controlled low-intensity fires that reduce wildfire risks while managing vegetation and promoting biodiversity.

The scale of indigenous land stewardship is substantial. Indigenous peoples manage around 25% of the world's land, containing significant biodiversity and carbon stores. They provide environmental stewardship to at least 36% of the world's intact forests. Research demonstrates that "many forests and other ecosystems within areas managed by Indigenous Peoples are in better health than nature outside these areas."

The article is emphatic about the Paris Agreement: "It is impossible to achieve the goals of the Paris Agreement without full and effective participation of Indigenous Peoples and their free, prior and informed consent." At COP26, leaders acknowledged indigenous peoples' vital role, and governments pledged $1.7 billion to support their efforts, though more funding remains necessary.

Despite their crucial contributions, indigenous peoples face substantial barriers. They are "frequently excluded from decision-making processes" on environmental policies, and "their rights to lands and resources are not always respected." This marginalisation stems from historical power imbalances, leaving these communities disproportionately vulnerable to climate impacts while preventing effective participation in solutions.

The article recommends five government actions: recognise and respect indigenous land rights; establish legal frameworks for Free, Prior, and Informed Consent (FPIC); integrate indigenous knowledge into policy frameworks at multiple governance levels; support education and capacity building that preserves indigenous heritage; and provide direct climate finance access.

UNDP has invested approximately $163 million through its Small Grants Programme across 5,832 projects benefiting indigenous communities, alongside UN-REDD programmes and the Equator Initiative.
