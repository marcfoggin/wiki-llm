## Summary

This comprehensive 2022 report from the ICCA Consortium documents the diversity, status, and threats to indigenous peoples' and local communities' conserved territories (ICCAs / territories of life) across West and Central Asia and the Caucasus. Authored by Aibek Samakov and Marc Foggin with regional ICCA Consortium members, the report covers a region of 13.3 million km² spanning 20+ countries with 600 million people, extending from the Mediterranean and Black Seas to China and Mongolia.

The report begins with foundational definitions of ICCAs as territories where indigenous or local communities have a deep connection to the land, make and enforce governance decisions, and contribute to conservation of nature and their own well-being. It distinguishes among **defined ICCAs** (currently meeting all three criteria), **disrupted ICCAs** (where governance or access has been severed), and **desired ICCAs** (communities intentionally developing new conservation relationships with land). 

The core contribution is a typology of 16 documented case studies grouped by ecosystem: grasslands & mountains (6 cases), deserts & arid lands (3), forests & shrublands (3), wetlands & rivers (2), and coastal areas (2). These span Iran, Iraq, Lebanon, Turkey, Armenia, Kyrgyzstan, Kazakhstan, Tajikistan, and Afghanistan. Cases include mobile pastoralists (Iran, Turkey, Kyrgyzstan), mountain farming (Tajikistan), ecovillages (Armenia), kariz irrigation systems (Iran–Xinjiang), al-hima reserves (Lebanon), walnut forests (Kyrgyzstan), and marsh arab riparian livelihoods (Iraq–Iran).

The report emphasises that ICCAs contribute significantly to global biodiversity conservation: 40% of formally protected areas overlap indigenous lands; ICCAs cover 22% of global Key Biodiversity Areas. In West and Central Asia specifically, five of the world's 34 biodiversity hotspots are located in the region (Mountains of Central Asia, Caucasus, Irano-Anatolian, eastern Mediterranean, Horn of Africa). The region also contains major geographic features — Tianshan, Pamir, Caucasus, and Hindukush mountains; steppes and grasslands; deserts (Kyzyl-Kum, Taklamakan, Dashte-Lut); and riparian ecosystems of critical international rivers.

The report documents historical disruptions to pastoral ICCAs: Soviet-era forced sedentarisation (1928–1934), Iranian nationalisation decrees (1963) that required tribal sedentarisation, and post-colonial border creation that fragmented nomadic territories. It notes that nomadic pastoralism in the region has roots ~12,000 years ago when ancestors learned seasonal migration from wild ungulates and then domesticated herds. Modern threats include armed conflicts (Syria, Afghanistan, Iraq, Yemen), large-scale infrastructure projects, climate change, and ongoing sedentarisation policies that continue after the Soviet period. The report also highlights the transboundary nature of many pastoral ICCAs: borders between Iran–Turkmenistan, Iran–Afghanistan, and at the tripoint of Afghanistan–Tajikistan–Uzbekistan separate seasonal pastures and migration corridors.

Finally, the report presents three clusters of recommendations: (A) documentation of ICCAs and country-level legal reviews; (B) self-strengthening (recognition, positioning in global biodiversity frameworks, supporting programmes, official IUCN governance classification); and (C) international action (visibility in IPBES/IPCC, integration into climate and biodiversity dialogues, Free Prior Informed Consent for development projects).

## Key Claims

- Indigenous peoples and local communities in West and Central Asia govern and conserve vast, diverse territories encompassing all geoclimatic and sociopolitical regions, often intricately linked to cultural identity and spiritual values
- Nomadic pastoralism evolved ~12,000 years ago in the region and remains a highly adapted livelihood for variable environments; seasonal migration is essential for sustainable use of pastures and water
- Modern state borders (many created post-1991 after Soviet collapse) have fragmented pastoral territories, restricting access to seasonal pastures and disrupting centuries-old migration corridors
- Soviet forced sedentarisation (1928–1934) and Iranian nationalisation decrees (1963) caused lasting loss of pastoral lands; sedentarisation policies continue in the post-Soviet period
- Five of the world's 34 biodiversity hotspots are fully or partially embedded in West and Central Asia; ICCAs in the region overlap significantly with these hotspots and with formally protected areas
- Indigenous and local governance systems in the region include al-hima (Arabian Peninsula), agdal (North Africa), tagal (West Africa), qoroq (Central Asia), and sacred sites, each with distinct cultural and ecological roles
- Traditional ecological knowledge systems in the region include sophisticated understanding of seasonal grazing patterns, water sources, livestock health, weather indicators, and ecological dynamics
- Protected area governance varies: some protected areas (e.g., Yagnob National Park) successfully co-manage with local communities; others restrict access to territories traditionally managed by pastoralists
- COVID-19 pandemic disrupted access of local communities to their territories and livelihoods in many parts of the region
- Armed conflicts in Caucasus, Middle East, and Central Asia pose immense threats to ICCAs and local communities
- Large-scale infrastructural projects planned for the region (e.g., dams, roads, mining) have potential to negatively impact ICCAs; Free Prior Informed Consent (FPIC) from affected communities is essential

## Key Concepts & Definitions

**ICCA (Territories of Life)** — Areas and territories conserved by indigenous peoples and local communities through functioning governance institutions and management practices that contribute to conservation of nature and community well-being. The terms are synonymous.

**Three Defining Characteristics of ICCAs:**
1. Deep connection between territory and indigenous/local community (rooted in history, identity, spirituality, material/non-material well-being)
2. Community makes and enforces key governance decisions through functioning institutions
3. Governance decisions and management contribute to conservation and community well-being

**Three Statuses of ICCAs:**
- **Defined** — meeting all three characteristics
- **Disrupted** — one or two characteristics severed due to external circumstances (forced removal, boundary changes, etc.)
- **Desired** — newly created or reconstituted communities consciously working toward developing ICCAs based on shared values

**Pastoralism Patterns:**
- **Horizontal migration** — long-distance movement across steppes
- **Vertical/altitudinal migration** — seasonal movement up and down mountains

## Geographic Diversity

**Region covers 13.3 million km² with 8 main ecological/cultural sub-regions:**

1. **Arabian Peninsula** — desert, subsistence farming, some pastoralism
2. **Sinai Peninsula** — desert
3. **Mediterranean Basin** (eastern/northern portions) — one of 34 global biodiversity hotspots
4. **Fertile Crescent** — ancient agricultural centre (Mesopotamia, Levant)
5. **Anatolia (Turkey)** — mountains, diverse ecosystems
6. **South Caucasus** — Mountains, diverse ecology; historically influenced by Russian Empire/Soviet Union
7. **Iranian Plateau** — diverse elevation, climate; historically influenced by Persian Empire
8. **Central Asia** — Steppes, mountains (Tianshan, Pamir, Hindukush), deserts, riparian ecosystems; historically influenced by Soviet Union

**Major Geographic Features:**
- Mountain ranges: Tianshan, Pamir, Caucasus, Hindukush
- Deserts: Kyzyl-Kum, Taklamakan, Dashte-Lut
- Arid/semi-arid climate prone to drought in many sub-regions
- Riparian ecosystems: Tigris–Euphrates, Amu Darya, Syr Darya
- Water bodies: Mediterranean, Persian Gulf, Black Sea, Red Sea, Caspian Sea, Aral Sea, Ysyk-Köl, Balkhash
- The Aral Sea represents a man-made environmental catastrophe (shrunk to 10% of original size due to irrigation diversion)

**Linguistic Diversity:**
- Semitic (Arabic, Hebrew)
- Indo-European (Iranian, Slavic languages)
- Turkic (Kyrgyz, Kazakh, etc.)

**Religious Diversity:**
- Islam (Sunni, Shia)
- Christianity (predominantly Orthodox)
- Local beliefs and practices pre-dating major religions

## Case Studies: 16 ICCA Examples

### Grasslands & Mountains (6 cases)

1. **Mobile pastoralists of Iran** — Tribal confederacies (Qashqai, Bakhtiari, Shahsevan, Sangsari, Bahmai) practising seasonal transhumance; many have lost winter grounds to agricultural development; disputes ongoing
2. **Sarıkeçili Yörüks (Turkey)** — Pastoral communities in Taurus Mountains; face restrictions from protected areas and development pressure
3. **Pastoralism in Tianshan Mountains (Kyrgyzstan)** — High-altitude pastoral territories; kyrgyz herders maintain traditional knowledge of seasonal migration
4. **Mountain farming in Yagnob Valley (Tajikistan)** — Isolated mountain community with distinct language and traditional agricultural practices; overlaps with Yagnob National Park
5. **Ecovillages in Armenia** — Communities developing new sustainable livelihood models
6. **Baiboosun community reserve (Kyrgyzstan)** — Community-led conservation initiative

### Deserts & Other Arid Lands (3 cases)

7. **Kariz irrigation system (Iran to Xinjiang)** — Ancient underground aqueduct network spanning centuries and multiple countries; exemplifies transboundary traditional water management
8. **Territories of life in Wakhi region (Pamir Mountains)** — Communities in high-altitude ecosystems of the Pamir Mountains
9. **Al-Hima system (Lebanon)** — Traditional reserve/sanctuary system with ancient roots in Islamic law; governs grazing, water, and resource use

### Forests & Shrublands (3 cases)

10. **Tahtacı Türkmens (Turkey)** — "Forest nomads" in Kaz Mountains; rights not sufficiently recognised; practise transhumant pastoralism integrated with forest management
11. **Walnut forests (Kyrgyzstan)** — Communities managing walnut and fruit tree forests in Tianshan Mountains; traditional resource use integrated with conservation
12. **Saphari forests (Georgia)** — Western Lesser Caucasus Mountains; community-managed forest territory

### Wetlands & Rivers (2 cases)

13. **Riparian communities (Kazakhstan)** — Syr Darya River; traditional livelihoods tied to riparian ecosystems
14. **Marsh Arabs (Iraq & Iran)** — Tigris–Euphrates marshlands; traditional reed-based livelihoods, water management, and spiritual practices; severely disrupted by dam construction and sedentarisation policies; recent partial restoration efforts

### Coastal Areas (2 cases)

15. **Traditional fishermen (Turkey)** — Coastal lagoon communities; traditional small-scale fisheries
16. **Sacred sites (Kyrgyzstan)** — Ysyk-Köl Biosphere Reserve; sites of spiritual and cultural significance to local communities; not formally recognised in state frameworks

## Historical Context: Pastoralism in West and Central Asia

**Origins (~12,000 years ago):**
- Ancestors were hunter-gatherers; learned seasonal migration from wild ungulates (mountain sheep, goats)
- Domesticated these animals and the dog, enabling management of large herds during migrations
- Evolved in region between Zagros Mountains (western Iran) and Levant (eastern Mediterranean)

**Soviet Period (1922–1991):**
- Territories in Central Asia became part of Soviet Union
- Forced sedentarisation of nomadic peoples (1928–1934) — abolition of private property, forced collectivisation
- Nomadic territories converted to agricultural land
- Sedentarisation policies continued after Soviet collapse

**Iran (Post-1963):**
- Natural resources declared national property by 1963 Nationalisation decree
- Sedentarisation required of tribal confederacies (Qashqai, Bakhtiari, Shahsevan, Sangsari, Bahmai)
- Many tribes lost seasonal pastures (e.g., Shahsevan's winter grounds) to development
- Land disputes ongoing

**Border Creation & Transboundary Fragmentation:**
- Colonial and post-colonial border creation disrupted pastoral territories
- Modern borders (especially post-1991 Soviet collapse) separated seasonal pastures and migration corridors
- Examples of fragmented territories:
  - Iran–Turkmenistan border
  - Iran–Afghanistan border
  - Afghanistan–Tajikistan–Uzbekistan tripoint
- Armed conflicts (Syria, Afghanistan, Iraq, Yemen) further restrict access and pose threats

## Major Threats to ICCAs in the Region

1. **Border restrictions & transboundary fragmentation** — Pastoral territories split by international borders; herders cannot access seasonal pastures
2. **Armed conflicts** — Caucasus, Middle East, Central Asia; displace communities, restrict access, destroy governance institutions
3. **Large-scale infrastructure projects** — Dams, roads, mining; have major direct and indirect impacts on ICCAs
4. **Sedentarisation policies** — Government pressures for fixed residence continue post-Soviet period
5. **Climate change** — Accelerates biodiversity loss, land degradation, resource scarcity; particularly threatens arid/semi-arid rangelands
6. **COVID-19 pandemic** — Government restrictions disrupted access to territories and traditional livelihoods
7. **Protected area restrictions** — Rules sometimes significantly limit community access to traditional territories
8. **Privatisation of commons** — Land privatisation undermines collective governance

## Opportunities for ICCAs in the Region

1. **Legal recognition & frameworks** — Country-level legal reviews; formal recognition of IUCN governance classifications
2. **ICCA documentation** — Participatory, community-led documentation of territories and traditional practices
3. **Integration into global frameworks** — Position ICCAs in SDGs 14 & 15, post-2020 Biodiversity Framework, NBSAP-II, IPBES assessments
4. **Co-management of protected areas** — Collaborative governance models that respect rights and traditional use
5. **Free Prior Informed Consent (FPIC)** — Ensure communities participate in assessment and planning for development projects
6. **International support programmes** — UNDP Global Support Initiative, Small Grants Programme; donor support at policy and practical levels
7. **Transboundary cooperation** — Regional dialogue to address fragmented pastoral territories

## Traditional Governance Systems Mentioned

- **Al-Hima** — Islamic-rooted reserve/sanctuary system for managing grazing, water, wildlife in Arabian Peninsula and Levant
- **Agdal** — Similar system in North Africa; traditional reserve with seasonal access rules
- **Tagal** — West African version of similar practice
- **Qoroq** — Central Asian governance system for pastoral territories
- **Yerli Qorukh** — Regional variant
- **Yiyk Jer** — Local governance term
- **Sacred sites & ancestral domains** — Known by local names; spiritual and cultural significance

## Global Context: ICCAs and Biodiversity

**Global ICCA statistics:**
- 21% of world's land area = "potential ICCAs"
- 25% of world's land area = estimated indigenous-managed lands (370M indigenous people)
- Up to 50% of world's land = either owned or managed by indigenous and local communities (including when LCs counted)
- Over 40% of formally protected areas globally overlap indigenous peoples' lands (Garnett et al. 2018)
- 26% of state- and privately-governed protected areas overlap potential ICCAs (UNEP-WCMC & ICCA Consortium 2021)
- 22% of global Key Biodiversity Areas (KBAs) covered by estimated ICCAs
- In regions with indigenous land management, biodiversity declining less rapidly than in other lands (IPBES 2019)

**Five Biodiversity Hotspots in West and Central Asia:**
1. Mountains of Central Asia
2. Caucasus Mountains
3. Irano-Anatolian region
4. Eastern and northern Mediterranean Basin
5. Horn of Africa

## Key Quotes

> "Indigenous peoples and local communities govern and conserve vast territories of life across the breadth of West and Central Asia and the Caucasus. These territories are found in all geoclimatic and sociopolitical regions and are highly diverse in their ecological and cultural characteristics. However, in nearly all instances, people and land are intricately connected through people's livelihoods and their territories of life are intertwined with culture and sense of identity."

> "Nomadism as a way of life evolved approximately 12,000 years ago in the area between the Zagros Mountains (in today's western Iran) and the mountains of the Levant (the eastern part of the Mediterranean)... Our ancestors learned seasonal migration and nomadism from these wild animals. Some 12,000 years ago, they began to domesticate these wild animals, and eventually also the dog, which allowed for the management of large herds of livestock during the migrations in the tribal territories." — Taghi Farvar, Section on Historical Perspectives on Pastoralism

> "Although in some areas (e.g. Iran) local communities have been documenting and mapping their own ICCAs for some time, in other areas (e.g. Kyrgyzstan, Lebanon, Armenia, Turkey, etc.) the documentation process has been driven more by local scholars and NGOs. Overall, the diversity and current status of ICCAs across the region remain relatively understudied."

> "There is no single one-size-fits-all solution for promoting recognition of ICCAs across the region, as these institutions are extremely diverse across institutional, historical, political and cultural perspectives."

## Recommendations from Report (Condensed)

**A. Documentation:**
1. Launch/continue ICCA documentation and mapping; state of knowledge is uneven across region
2. Country-level legal reviews of regulations, policies, legal gaps, barriers, opportunities
3. Involve indigenous peoples and local communities in knowledge co-production and research

**B. Self-Strengthening:**
4. Increase efforts to promote meaningful recognition and strengthen IPLIC rights (support from civil society, government, intergovernmental bodies)
5. Position ICCAs in global/national dialogues on biodiversity (SDGs 14 & 15, NBSAP-II, post-2020 targets)
6. Promote IUCN Matrix recognition (includes ICCAs as governance type)
7. Promote through complementary concepts (OECMs, UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage)
8. Support programmes: UNDP Global Support Initiative, Small Grants Programme, UNEP, UNESCO; advocate donor support

**C. International Action:**
9. Raise visibility in international science-policy platforms (IPBES, IPCC, Coalition for Nature, GEO, Global Land Outlook)
10. All climate and biodiversity dialogues must consider impacts on ICCAs and IPLCs
11. Large-scale infrastructure projects: enable IPLC participation at early planning stages
12. **Free Prior Informed Consent (FPIC)** must be obtained from communities for projects affecting ICCAs
