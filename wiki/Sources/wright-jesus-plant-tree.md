## Summary

N.T. Wright argues that Western Christianity has fundamentally misunderstood eschatology, wrongly emphasising a disembodied heaven separated from earth rather than God's promised renewal of creation itself. This theological error has led many Christians to dismiss environmental care as spiritually irrelevant — some arguing "there was no point in worrying about the trees and acid rain" because Jesus would soon destroy the present world. Wright directly challenges this as theologically unfounded.

The core of his counterargument rests on early Christian teaching about bodily resurrection and cosmic renewal. Rather than the world being discarded, God will recreate it, transforming rather than abandoning the physical universe. Jesus's resurrection models this: "Jesus didn't leave his body behind in the tomb and grow a new one" but was raised bodily with recognisable marks. This demonstrates God's commitment to physical creation rather than escape from it.

Wright resolves the tension between present work and future renewal by distinguishing between building *of* versus building *for* the kingdom. Christians cannot establish God's final kingdom through their own efforts — that will be "the free gift of God, a massive act of grace." But they are commissioned to construct *toward* that kingdom, like craftsmen carving individual stones for a cathedral without seeing the final design. This gives present environmental and social work genuine significance.

The theological foundation centres on the doctrine of creation paired with divine judgement. Taking both seriously means God will judge and renew creation rather than destroy it, producing "new heavens and new earth" from the old rather than from nothing. The resurrection launched God's new creation immediately, making Christians "agents" of that renewal today.

Wright concludes that Christian environmental work — planting trees, pursuing justice, creating art — is not ultimately futile but will be "enhanced in God's new world." This transforms ecology from optional concern to essential kingdom work grounded in Christian hope.

## Key Claims

- The "it's all going to burn" argument is theologically unfounded — God renews creation, not discards it
- Bodily resurrection demonstrates God's commitment to the physical world
- Christians build *for* the kingdom, not *of* it — present work has genuine eternal significance
- Environmental stewardship is essential kingdom work, not an optional extra
