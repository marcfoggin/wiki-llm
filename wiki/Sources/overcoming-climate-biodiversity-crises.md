## Summary

Published in *Science* (2023), this major review develops a roadmap toward sustainability by examining the links among climate, biodiversity, and society. Earth's biodiversity and human societies face multiple interacting threats: pollution, overconsumption of natural resources, urbanisation, demographic shifts, social and economic inequalities, and habitat loss — many of which are exacerbated by climate change.

The authors propose a dual target: limiting warming to 1.5°C and effectively conserving and restoring functional ecosystems on 30–50% of land, freshwater, and ocean areas. They envision a mosaic of interconnected protected and shared spaces, including intensively used spaces, to strengthen self-sustaining biodiversity, the capacity of people and nature to adapt to and mitigate climate change, and nature's contributions to people.

The key argument is that climate and biodiversity crises are deeply coupled — solutions for one can harm the other if pursued in isolation. For example, bioenergy plantations may help climate targets but devastate biodiversity. Conversely, well-designed nature-based solutions can address both simultaneously. The paper calls for fostering interlinked human, ecosystem, and planetary health approaches.

This has direct relevance for pastoral and mountain contexts, where climate adaptation, biodiversity conservation, and livelihoods intersect in the same landscapes.

## Key Claims

- Climate and biodiversity crises must be tackled together — siloed solutions risk making one worse
- 30–50% of land, freshwater, and ocean should be effectively conserved and restored
- A mosaic of interconnected protected and shared spaces is needed, not just fortress reserves
- Nature-based solutions can address climate and biodiversity simultaneously when well-designed
