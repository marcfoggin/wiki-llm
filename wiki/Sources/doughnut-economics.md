## Summary

Kate Raworth introduced the Doughnut concept in a 2012 Oxfam report, which "rapidly gained traction internationally, from the Pope and the UN General Assembly to Extinction Rebellion." Her 2017 book *Doughnut Economics: Seven Ways to Think Like a 21st Century Economist* expanded on these ideas and became an international bestseller published in over 20 languages.

The Doughnut consists of two concentric rings representing boundaries for human flourishing. The inner ring is the social foundation — life's essentials that ensure no one falls short in areas like food, water, health, and education. The outer ring is the ecological ceiling, protecting planetary systems from destabilisation. Between these boundaries lies the "safe and just space" where humanity can thrive.

The framework fundamentally reframes economic goals away from endless GDP growth toward meeting all people's needs within planetary limits. Rather than prescribing specific policies, it proposes "a way of thinking to bring about the regenerative and distributive dynamics that this century calls for," drawing from ecological, feminist, institutional, behavioural, and complexity economics. It recognises human nature as cooperative and caring, and economies as complex interdependent systems requiring systems thinking.

"Regenerative and distributive by design" addresses organisational transformation through five key layers: Purpose, Networks, Governance, Ownership, and Finance. Organisations embracing this approach seek to "work with and within the cycles of the living world" while redistributing power and value among all stakeholders who contributed to creation.

DEAL (Doughnut Economics Action Lab) established eight guiding principles for implementation: embracing the 21st-century goal, seeing the big picture, nurturing human nature, thinking systemically, being distributive, being regenerative, aiming to thrive rather than grow, and being strategic. The framework has been adopted globally, with DEAL running transformational workshops across enterprises, city departments, foundations, and organisations. The Doughnut has evolved into "a convening space for big conversations about reimagining and remaking the future."

## Key Claims

- GDP growth is an inadequate measure of progress; it ignores ecological degradation and social inequality
- The safe space for humanity lies between a social foundation and an ecological ceiling
- Economies should be regenerative and distributive by design, not dependent on growth
- Human nature is cooperative and caring, not merely self-interested as classical economics assumes
- Systems thinking is essential — economies are complex, interdependent systems
