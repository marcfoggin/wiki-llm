## Summary

This comprehensive overview, published in *Ecosphere*, challenges the systematic undervaluation of grassland ecosystems. Extensively managed grasslands are recognised globally for their high biodiversity and their social and cultural values, yet their capacity to deliver multiple ecosystem services as parts of agricultural systems is surprisingly understudied compared to other production systems.

Using southern Africa and northwest Europe as case studies, the authors demonstrate that natural and semi-natural grasslands supply far more than agricultural products. They provide critical non-agricultural services including water supply and flow regulation, carbon storage, erosion control, climate mitigation, pollination, and cultural ecosystem services.

The paper finds that while demand for ecosystem services seems to balance supply in natural grasslands of southern Africa, the situation in northwest Europe is more precarious due to agricultural intensification. The key message is that grasslands deserve conservation attention comparable to forests — they cover vast areas, sustain significant biodiversity, and underpin multiple ecosystem services that are poorly recognised in policy frameworks.

This matters for pastoral contexts because rangelands — which are predominantly grassland ecosystems — are home to hundreds of millions of pastoralists whose livelihoods depend on the very services this paper documents.

## Key Claims

- Grasslands provide ecosystem services comparable to forests but receive a fraction of conservation attention
- Key services include water regulation, carbon storage, erosion control, climate mitigation, and pollination
- Agricultural intensification threatens grassland ecosystem services, particularly in Europe
- Policy frameworks systematically undervalue grassland contributions
