## Summary

The ICCA Consortium is an international association of Indigenous Peoples, local communities, and supporting organisations dedicated to documenting, sustaining, and defending ICCAs — territories of life. ICCAs are territories and areas conserved by Indigenous peoples and local communities through customary governance and management.

The Consortium works across the intersection of indigenous rights and conservation, advocating for recognition that community-governed territories are among the most biodiverse and ecologically intact areas on the planet. Its activities span documentation of territories of life worldwide, advocacy in international policy forums, legal support for community land rights, and capacity building for community governance.

A particular focus is aquatic territories of life — from oceans to rivers and lakes — taken care of by indigenous and traditional communities. The Consortium also documents specific cases, from the Tanabag Batak tribe's ICCA in the Philippines to the Guassa Afro-alpine moorland in Ethiopia's Central Highlands.

The 2024–2028 Strategic Plan guides the Consortium's work, with priorities including strengthening community governance, defending against threats to territories of life, and ensuring indigenous and community perspectives shape global conservation policy — particularly in the context of the Kunming-Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework's 30x30 target.

## Key Claims

- ICCAs (territories of life) are among the most biodiverse areas on the planet
- Community governance is often more effective at conservation than state-managed approaches
- Indigenous and community territories must be recognised in the 30x30 framework, not overridden by it
- Both terrestrial and aquatic territories of life require recognition and protection
