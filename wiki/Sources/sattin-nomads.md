## Summary

Anthony Sattin argues that nomadic peoples have been central to human civilisation rather than peripheral to it. Settled societies have historically viewed nomads as marginal, backward, or threatening — but this framing obscures the enormous contributions mobile communities have made to human development.

Sattin demonstrates that nomadic communities catalysed major developments across history: from the first stone monuments at Gobekli Tepe to the Mongol empires that facilitated the Silk Roads and ultimately enabled the European Renaissance. Trade, cultural exchange, and technological diffusion were driven by mobile peoples connecting distant settled populations.

The book contends that settled civilisation's bias against nomadism is not merely historical prejudice but an ongoing political and cultural force that justifies dispossession, sedentarisation, and the marginalisation of mobile peoples today.

Sattin concludes that modern civilisation would benefit from adopting nomadic perspectives on flexibility, sustainability, and living in harmony with nature — values that are increasingly relevant in an era of climate change and ecological crisis.

## Key Claims

- Nomadic peoples catalysed major civilisational developments, from Gobekli Tepe to the Silk Roads
- The settled/nomadic binary is a political construction that justifies dispossession
- Modern societies can learn from nomadic values: flexibility, sustainability, ecological harmony
- Anti-nomadic bias remains an active force in policy and culture today
