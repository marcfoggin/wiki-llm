## Summary

Thaddeus Williams argues that while the Bible does not use the term "systemic," Scripture clearly addresses injustice embedded in structures and laws. He defines biblically-grounded systemic injustice as "any system that either requires or encourages those within the system to break the moral laws God revealed for his creatures' flourishing." Biblical examples include Egypt's enslavement of the Jews and nations permitting child sacrifice; historical examples include American slavery and Jim Crow segregation. The Bible calls Christians not merely to personal piety but to demonstrate God's justice "more radiantly in the systems of earth as it is in heaven."

Williams sharply distinguishes between two approaches to identifying systemic injustice. He critiques the trending definition that automatically equates unequal outcomes with systemic discrimination, referencing Ibram X. Kendi's assertion that "racial disparities must be the result of racial discrimination." This three-step framework — spotting inequality, interpreting it as evidence of discrimination, overthrowing the system — often becomes a fourth step in churches, where disagreement is branded as complicity with "white supremacy or patriarchal oppression."

To illustrate, Williams presents cases where disparities have alternative explanations. A New Jersey Turnpike speeding study revealed that Black drivers represented 25% of speeders despite comprising 16% of drivers — explained by younger demographic age correlating with speeding behaviour. Mortgage rejection rates between racial groups appear less significant when considering that Black-owned banks turned down Black applicants at a higher rate than white-owned banks. White Americans rank sixteenth in median household income among ancestry groups, with Indian, Chinese, Korean, and Nigerian communities earning more on average.

Psychologically, Williams suggests humans naturally prefer damning explanations because they offer moral melodrama and heroic purpose. He references C.S. Lewis's warning that seeking damning conclusions about opponents leads to a "universe of pure hatred."

Williams concludes by recommending intellectual humility and exposure to diverse perspectives. He encourages reading authors like Thomas Sowell, consulting marginalised voices that question dominant frameworks, and then examining opposing viewpoints. This balanced approach allows believers to authentically distinguish "real racism, real sexism, and other real vicious isms" from spurious claims, thereby taking discrimination and its victims "seriously enough."

## Key Claims

- The Bible addresses systemic injustice even without using the term — sin can be expressed through systems
- Automatically equating disparity with discrimination dulls our ability to identify actual injustice
- Multiple factors explain disparities: age, geography, personal choices, cultural patterns
- Intellectual humility and diverse perspectives are essential for discerning real injustice
- Christians are called to demonstrate God's justice in systems, not only in personal behaviour
