## Summary

This FAO handbook calls for the legal recognition and securing of pastoral mobility as a way of safeguarding and facilitating a continuous stream of economic and social benefits for pastoralists, countries, and the environment. It is a practical guide for governments and policy-makers.

The handbook facilitates a deeper understanding of pastoral mobility through examples drawn from diverse legal and policy contexts worldwide. It addresses the gap between the ecological and economic logic of pastoral mobility and the legal frameworks that typically govern land and natural resources, which are usually designed for settled populations.

Key themes include: the rationale for legally recognising pastoral mobility; existing legal frameworks and their gaps; the relationship between customary tenure and statutory law; cross-border mobility and the need for bilateral or regional agreements; and practical steps for developing or reforming national legal frameworks.

The handbook argues that legal recognition of mobility is not merely a rights issue but an economic and environmental imperative. When pastoral mobility is restricted through legal or administrative means, the result is typically ecological degradation, livelihood collapse, and increased conflict — outcomes that are worse for everyone, not only pastoralists.

## Key Claims

- Pastoral mobility must be legally recognised and secured — it is an economic and environmental imperative
- Current legal frameworks are designed for settled populations and systematically disadvantage pastoralists
- Cross-border mobility requires bilateral or regional agreements
- Restricting mobility leads to ecological degradation, livelihood collapse, and conflict
