## Summary

John Stott addresses a fundamental tension in Christian mission by arguing that the Great Commission and Great Commandment are distinct yet complementary imperatives. Rather than treating evangelism as a substitute for loving one's neighbour, he insists these represent two separate obligations. "The Great Commission neither explains, nor exhausts, nor supersedes the Great Commandment" — sharing the gospel adds a spiritual dimension to neighbour-love rather than replacing it.

Stott's resolution centres on understanding the human person holistically. Genuine neighbour-love demands concern for the complete welfare of others: spiritual, physical, and communal. Since God created humans as "body-soul-in-community," Christians must address all three dimensions. This allows evangelism to coexist with social service — Christians should share their faith, but they cannot ethically stop there if they truly care for their neighbours' wellbeing.

The argument extends into structural concerns. Stott maintains that loving neighbours requires not merely treating the afflicted but pursuing systemic change — "preventive medicine or community health." Christians should work toward "better social structures in which peace, dignity, freedom, and justice are secured for all." He permits collaboration with non-Christians in these pursuits, demonstrating that social justice work stands independent of explicitly Christian motivation.

Stott ultimately characterises evangelism and social action as "partners" in God's mission. Each constitutes "an end in itself" rather than serving as a means to the other. Both flow from authentic love, requiring no additional justification. Christians are "sent into the world, like Jesus, to serve," expressing love through proclamation and compassionate action alike.

## Key Claims

- The Great Commission does not supersede the Great Commandment — both are binding obligations
- Humans are body-soul-in-community; genuine love addresses all three dimensions
- Social justice work is an end in itself, not merely a means to evangelism
- Christians may collaborate with non-Christians in pursuing structural justice
- Evangelism and social action are partners, each valid in its own right
