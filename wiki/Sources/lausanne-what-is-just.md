## Summary

This Lausanne report examines justice as central to effective global Christian mission and witness. It begins from the premise that all humans bear God's image and deserve equal treatment, yet acknowledges that "globally, not all humans are treated equally."

The report addresses interconnected dimensions of justice: poverty reduction (documenting remarkable progress alongside persistent challenges), religious persecution (growing state restrictions on religion), women's participation in church leadership, marginalisation of people with disabilities, human rights setbacks, modern slavery, and corruption.

It documents both progress and regression — extreme poverty has been dramatically reduced in recent decades, yet corruption remains endemic in many contexts, and religious freedom is declining in numerous countries. The report frames equitable treatment as inseparable from authentic Christian witness.

The significance for pastoral and development contexts is that justice issues intersect — pastoralist marginalisation involves land rights, service provision gaps, governance exclusion, and cultural erasure, all of which this report addresses as interconnected dimensions of a single justice agenda.

## Key Claims

- All humans bear God's image and deserve equal treatment — justice is a theological imperative
- Extreme poverty has been dramatically reduced but persistent challenges remain
- Religious freedom is declining globally with growing state restrictions
- Justice is multi-dimensional: poverty, persecution, gender, disability, slavery, corruption
- Equitable treatment is inseparable from authentic Christian witness
