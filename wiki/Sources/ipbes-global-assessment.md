## Summary

The IPBES Global Assessment (2019) is the most comprehensive intergovernmental assessment of the state of biodiversity and ecosystem services ever conducted. Prepared by 145 expert authors from 50 countries over three years, with inputs from 310 contributing authors, it assesses changes over the past five decades and provides a foundation for policy action.

The assessment found that nature is declining globally at rates unprecedented in human history. Around 1 million animal and plant species are threatened with extinction, many within decades — more than ever before in human history. The current rate of global species extinction is tens to hundreds of times higher than the average over the past 10 million years.

Five direct drivers of change are identified, in order of impact: (1) changes in land and sea use, (2) direct exploitation of organisms, (3) climate change, (4) pollution, and (5) invasive alien species. These are in turn underpinned by societal values and behaviours including production and consumption patterns, human population dynamics, trade, and governance.

The assessment emphasises that goals for conserving and sustainably using nature cannot be met by current trajectories, and that fundamental, system-wide reorganisation is needed across technological, economic, and social factors. It is particularly relevant because it was the first global assessment to systematically integrate indigenous and local knowledge alongside scientific evidence.

## Key Claims

- Around 1 million species are threatened with extinction — many within decades
- The current extinction rate is tens to hundreds of times higher than the 10-million-year average
- Five direct drivers: land/sea use change, exploitation, climate change, pollution, invasive species
- Current trajectories will not meet conservation goals — fundamental system-wide change is needed
- First global assessment to systematically integrate indigenous and local knowledge
