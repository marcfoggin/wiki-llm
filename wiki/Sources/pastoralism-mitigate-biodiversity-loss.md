## Summary

Published in *BioScience* (2025), this paper makes a significant argument: sustainable pastoralism represents a primary strategy for supporting the goals of the Kunming–Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework. The authors provide compelling spatial evidence — 67% of biodiversity hotspots and 38% of key biodiversity areas globally include rangelands — yet international conventions seldom recognise this vast biodiversity repository.

The paper identifies four key synergies between pastoralism and biodiversity conservation:

1. **Working lands conservation** — pastoral rangelands managed for livestock production simultaneously maintain biodiversity habitat, unlike conversion to cropland or urban use
2. **Continuation of vital disturbance regimes** — grazing and burning by pastoral communities maintain grassland and savanna ecosystems that depend on periodic disturbance
3. **Connectivity through transhumance corridors** — seasonal pastoral migration routes function as ecological corridors connecting habitats across landscapes
4. **Community-led governance** — pastoral institutions for managing shared resources often align with biodiversity conservation objectives

The authors argue that leveraging these synergies offers critical opportunities to mitigate biodiversity loss through the creation of a vast conservation network that includes working lands and protected areas. This reframes pastoralism from a threat to biodiversity (as often perceived) to an active contributor to conservation.

## Key Claims

- 67% of biodiversity hotspots and 38% of key biodiversity areas include rangelands
- International conventions seldom recognise rangelands as a biodiversity repository
- Four specific synergies link pastoralism with biodiversity conservation
- Transhumance corridors function as ecological corridors — mobility supports connectivity
- Pastoral governance institutions often align with conservation objectives
