## Summary

Chris Wright's *Five Marks of Mission: Making God's Mission Ours* is a 40-page eBook from Micah Global's M-Series that provides authoritative theological grounding for the Five Marks framework adopted by the Lambeth Conference in 1988. Wright argues that understanding mission requires first understanding **God's mission** — the comprehensive plan of God to redeem all creation and bring unity to all things in and through Christ (Ephesians 1:9-10). 

The document establishes that "our mission is to participate with God in God's mission" — meaning the church's five dimensions of mission reflect what God himself does and wills. Wright anchors each mark in Christ's Lordship and links them to the Great Commission, arguing that all five marks together (not evangelism alone) constitute faithful discipleship and witness.

A key insight is Wright's grouping of the five marks into **three missional focal points**: (1) **Building the Church** through evangelism and teaching, (2) **Serving Society** through compassion and justice, and (3) **Caring for Creation** through stewardship. This structure emphasizes that Christian mission addresses simultaneously the spiritual renewal of believers, the social and political structures that affect human flourishing, and the ecological integrity of creation itself.

## Key Claims

- **God's mission is comprehensive**: To redeem and restore all creation—not just souls—through the cross and resurrection of Christ, fulfilling the biblical grand narrative from Creation through Fall, Redemption, and New Creation.

- **Our mission participates in God's mission**: The church is not inventing mission independently but joining God in what He is already doing. Mission means "going with God" into places where God is already at work.

- **The Five Marks are biblical at their core**: Each mark—evangelism, teaching, compassion, justice, creation care—can be shown to have deep roots throughout Scripture and represents areas where God has passionate concern.

- **All five marks depend on Christ's Lordship**: The entire framework holds together because all five dimensions affirm in different ways that Jesus Christ is Lord over all creation. This lordship is the integrating center.

- **Three focal points, not a hierarchy**: While the five marks can be grouped into three missional tasks (church, society, creation), none is subordinate to another. All are essential dimensions of faithful Christian presence in the world.

- **Holistic mission addresses systemic realities**: Building the church and serving society must together confront not just individual sin but unjust structures. Caring for creation recognizes that environmental degradation undermines human flourishing and the church's credibility.

- **Evangelism is framed within holistic mission, not above it**: Wright rejects the idea that the Five Marks replace or diminish the Great Commission. Rather, they fill out what "making disciples" actually entails in the context of God's full mission to redeem creation.

## Key Quotes

> "If the Bible shows us that God's great mission is so comprehensive in scope, then the church's mission must also be far-reaching and wide-ranging. Not, of course, in the sense that we can do all that God does; but in the sense that when God calls us to participate with him in fulfilling God's own great purpose for creation and humanity, he calls us into a very big agenda indeed."

> "Our mission is to participate with God in God's mission... Mission means 'joining with God', going with him, being called and taken by him, into the places where God is already at work and in the tasks where God is already engaged."

> "God is not an absentee general, issuing orders from heaven, but an active agent, sovereignly working out his purposes in history."

> "All of those five dimensions of mission depend on the Lordship of Christ. So here they are, linked together around the centrality of the gospel truth that Jesus is Lord."
