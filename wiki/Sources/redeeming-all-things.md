## Summary

Rachel Mander's *Redeeming All Things* (Micah Global, 2022) makes a comprehensive theological argument that creation care is not a trendy add-on to Christian mission but rather integral to the gospel itself. Writing for evangelical practitioners, Mander argues that creation care flows from recognizing Jesus as both Creator and Redeemer, understanding creation's inherent interdependence, and accepting that image-bearing—humanity's core calling—is fundamentally a missiological task centered on enabling creation to flourish.

The booklet challenges the church's complicity in ecological destruction and calls for urgent, prophetic ecological responsibility grounded in lament for what is lost. Mander deliberately connects creation care to the Five Marks of Mission framework, arguing that caring for creation is not distinct from proclamation, discipleship, service, and justice—but rather integral to each of them.

## Core Arguments

### 1. Jesus is Creator and Redeemer of All Things

Mander's foundation rests on Christology. Drawing on Colossians 1:15–20, she argues that Jesus is "the image of the invisible God, the firstborn over all creation," through whom "all things were created" and in whom "all things hold together." Jesus is simultaneously:

- **Creator**: "All creation is through him, for him, and reconciled in him"
- **Redeemer**: "The firstborn from among the dead, who repays, recovers and saves all things"

This means that Christ's Lordship—which Christians affirm in responding to the gospel—extends over all creation, not just humanity. As the Cape Town Commitment states: "To proclaim the gospel that says Jesus is Lord is to proclaim the gospel that includes the earth, since Christ's Lordship is over all creation. Creation care is thus a gospel issue within the Lordship of Jesus Christ."

**Key claim**: Redemption must be understood as "the reconciliation of all creation… under the reign of God" or, as she phrases it, "the kingdom of God is creation healed."

### 2. Creation's Interdependence Reflects God's Nature

Mander argues that creation is designed with inherent interdependence mirroring God's own relational nature (perichoresis—the communal relationship within the Trinity). Drawing on Richard Bauckham's concept of a "community of creation," she shows that:

- Genesis establishes both the independent value of each creature ("and it was good," Genesis 1) and their interdependent wholeness ("and it was all very good," Genesis 1:31)
- Scripture repeatedly affirms creation's participation in God's covenant: God's covenant with Noah includes all living things (Genesis 9:8–17); God's covenant with Israel involves their relationship with the land
- The Psalms present creation itself as worshipping God and proclaiming God's character
- God delights in creatures mysterious and dangerous to humans (Job 38–41) and sustains all life through breath, food, and habitat (Psalm 104)

Mander models this interdependence as a "triangle of relationships": God–humanity–earth. When sin enters, all three relationships fracture—with God (humanity hides), between humanity and earth (expulsion from Eden), and between God and earth (the ground is cursed). Yet God's restorative intention remains constant throughout Scripture, promising healing of these broken relationships.

### 3. Image-Bearing Is a Missiological Task

Humans are created in God's image (Genesis 1:27) to reflect God's character. For Mander, this image-bearing is not a passive status but an active calling—specifically a missiological one. Our "first great commission and clear job description" is to enable all creation to flourish.

She draws on Chris Wright's definition of mission: "our committed participation as God's people, at God's invitation and command, in God's own mission within the history of God's world for the redemption of God's creation." This moves beyond anthropocentric mission (focused solely on human evangelism) to recognize that God's mission encompasses all creation.

The Cape Town Commitment frames this as integral mission: "The gospel is God's good news… for individual persons, and for society, and for creation. All three are broken and suffering because of sin; all three are included in the redeeming love and mission of God; all three must be part of the comprehensive mission of God's people."

**Key implication**: Proclaiming the gospel without caring for creation is incomplete; it misrepresents who Jesus is and what the kingdom of God means.

### 4. The Church Is Complicit in Ecological Injustice

Mander unflinchingly names the church's role in ecological destruction. Drawing on Jonathan Moo, she states that Christians are called to be "instantiations of the kingdom of God, members of a resistance movement against all that would destroy God's creation"—yet often the church is complicit rather than resistant.

She outlines multiple forms of ecological injustice for which the church bears responsibility:

**Injustice through greed and exploitation**: We exploit creation and drive species to extinction, acting as careless stewards of what God entrusted to us (invoking Jeremiah 2:7). Biodiversity loss is an injustice in itself, regardless of its human impact.

**Injustice through production systems**: Manufacturing is concentrated in poor countries for wealthy consumers' benefit; profits flow to distant corporate headquarters while local communities bear environmental costs. Oil companies (Shell, BP, Total, Saudi Aramco, Exxon Mobil, Sinopec) extract resources thousands of miles from their headquarters, leaving devastation in their wake.

**Injustice through consumption**: The wealthy 10% of the global population produce half of global emissions, while the poorest bear the greatest consequences. Island nations (Tuvalu, Solomon Islands, Maldives, Kiribati, Fiji) face extinction from climate change they did not cause, without power to change other nations' trajectories.

**Injustice through inaction**: We remain "reluctant to really change," foreclosing futures for generations. As development expert Nazmul Chowdhury says: "Forget making poverty history. Climate change will make poverty permanent."

Mander emphasizes that this injustice is not simply about harm to people (though it is that); even if humans were not affected, creation care would be a missiological imperative—like Noah's calling to preserve all species (Genesis 7:3).

### 5. Lament Is Necessary and Prophetic

Mander argues that the church must move beyond guilt or activism to genuine lament—grieving death and destruction in all its forms, resonating with the biblical tradition of lament psalms. Lament is not despair but rather a turning toward God in the midst of suffering, modeled in Scripture and essential for authentic response.

This lament becomes the ground for prophetic ecological responsibility: speaking truth to power, refusing complicity, and calling for systemic change. Urgent ecological responsibility is both a theological imperative (rooted in God's character and Christ's lordship) and a missiological necessity (integral to the gospel proclamation we bear witness to).

## Key Claims

- Creation care is not a modern trend but a biblical rediscovery
- Jesus's lordship over creation is non-negotiable for authentic Christian faith
- Image-bearing humans are commissioned to enable all creation—not just other humans—to flourish
- Proclaiming the gospel without demonstrating it through creation care contradicts the gospel itself
- The church is complicit in ecological injustice and must respond prophetically
- Creation care integrates seamlessly with all five marks of mission (proclamation, discipleship, service, justice, stewardship)
- Integral mission means God's redemptive work encompasses individuals, society, *and* creation equally
