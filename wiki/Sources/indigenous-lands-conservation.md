## Summary

This landmark study published in *Nature Sustainability* is the first global aggregation of spatial information on Indigenous lands, filling a critical gap for conservation and climate policy. Using publicly available geospatial resources, the researchers demonstrate the sheer scale of indigenous land stewardship.

Indigenous Peoples manage or have tenure rights over at least ~38 million km² in 87 countries or politically distinct areas on all inhabited continents. This represents over a quarter of the world's land surface. Crucially, these lands intersect about 40% of all terrestrial protected areas and ecologically intact landscapes — including boreal and tropical primary forests, savannas, and marshes.

The results add to growing evidence that recognising Indigenous Peoples' rights to land, benefit sharing, and institutions is essential to meeting local and global conservation goals. The geospatial analysis indicates that collaborative partnerships involving conservation practitioners, Indigenous Peoples, and governments would yield significant benefits for conservation of ecologically valuable landscapes, ecosystems, and genes for future generations.

The study's significance lies in providing the first empirical, global-scale evidence that indigenous land management and conservation are not competing goals but deeply aligned ones. It has become a foundational reference for arguments linking indigenous rights with biodiversity outcomes.

## Key Claims

- Indigenous Peoples manage or have tenure over at least 38 million km² — over 25% of the world's land
- These lands intersect 40% of all terrestrial protected areas and ecologically intact landscapes
- Recognising indigenous rights to land is essential to meeting global conservation goals
- Collaborative partnerships between conservation, indigenous peoples, and governments yield the greatest benefits
