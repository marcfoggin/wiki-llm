## Summary

This ILRI synthesis compiles ten key facts that challenge the systematic undervaluation of rangeland ecosystems in global policy. Together, these facts make a compelling case that rangelands are among the most important yet neglected ecosystems on Earth.

1. **Global coverage** — 54% of the world's land surface consists of rangelands, spanning every continent except Antarctica
2. **Human evolution** — Humans developed key traits (flexible diets, large brains, bipedalism) while adapting to grassland landscapes in East Africa
3. **Human dependence** — Over 500 million people directly depend on rangelands; up to 2 billion benefit indirectly from ecosystem services
4. **Megafauna habitat** — African rangelands support the world's largest concentration of large mammals
5. **Ecological partnership** — Humans and livestock are essential components of healthy rangeland ecosystems that co-evolved over millennia
6. **Livestock feed** — More than 70% of the world's forage comes from rangeland landscapes
7. **Underground biodiversity** — Up to 75% of rangeland biomass exists below ground in root systems and soil organisms
8. **Carbon storage** — Rangelands contain nearly 30% of the world's terrestrial carbon, with 90% stored in soils
9. **Threatened ecosystems** — Approximately 49% of grasslands face degradation; some rangelands are disappearing faster than the Amazon rainforest
10. **Policy neglect** — Rangelands appear in only 10% of national climate plans, compared to 70% for forests

The contrast between the scale of rangeland importance (54% of land, 30% of carbon, 2 billion people) and the scale of policy neglect (10% of climate plans) is the core message.

## Key Claims

- Rangelands cover 54% of the world's land surface
- Nearly 30% of terrestrial carbon is stored in rangelands, 90% below ground
- 49% of grasslands face degradation — some faster than the Amazon
- Rangelands appear in only 10% of national climate plans vs 70% for forests
- Humans and livestock are co-evolved components of healthy rangeland ecosystems
