## Summary

The Evangelical Environmental Network (EEN) grounds creation care in biblical theology, asserting that environmental stewardship is a fundamental Christian obligation. The organisation emphasises that "Christ died to reconcile all of creation to God" and that "all of creation belongs to Jesus," establishing creation care as integral to worshiping and loving God. EEN explicitly rejects nature worship and pantheism, clarifying that only the Creator deserves worship — but insists that avoiding idolatry should not prevent Christians from their biblical duty to care for God's creation.

EEN's theological framework positions humans as having special responsibilities due to being created in God's image, yet not as separate from or superior to the rest of creation. God values all creation tremendously, as evidenced by Scripture describing creation as inherently good and destined for transformation in Christ's future kingdom. EEN teaches that "God created us to depend on the rest of his creation for our material existence," making environmental and economic stewardship deeply interconnected.

The network explicitly connects creation care to social justice and human health, recognising that "pollution hurts the poor the most" and that Christians are called to defend vulnerable populations. This extends the Great Commandment to love God and neighbours through environmental protection — it is impossible to genuinely love children while simultaneously exposing them to pollution and environmental harm.

EEN focuses on three primary initiatives: defending human health from environmental threats, protecting and enjoying creation, and championing energy prosperity. The organisation works through coalitions of evangelical churches and organisations to educate, disciple, and mobilise faith communities around creation stewardship. Their ultimate vision frames creation care as "a matter of life," embedding environmental responsibility within evangelical Christianity's central commitments.

## Key Claims

- Christ died to reconcile all of creation, not only humanity
- Humans have special stewardship responsibilities but are not separate from creation
- Pollution disproportionately harms the poor — creation care is a justice issue
- Creation care fulfils the Great Commandments to love God and love neighbours
- Environmental and economic stewardship are deeply interconnected
