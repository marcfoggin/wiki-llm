## Overview

Mainstream economic models often treat nature as an externality or unlimited input. This subtopic explores frameworks that challenge this assumption by integrating ecological limits and relational values into economic thinking. In mountain and pastoralist contexts, where livelihoods depend directly on ecosystem health, ignoring ecological boundaries leads to collapse rather than growth.

## Key Frameworks

### Doughnut Economics (Kate Raworth)
[About Doughnut Economics](https://doughnuteconomics.org/about-doughnut-economics) — A framework proposing two concentric boundaries: an inner social foundation (ensuring basic human needs) and an outer ecological ceiling (protecting planetary systems). The safe and just space for humanity lies between these rings. Rather than pursuing endless GDP growth, it calls for economies that are regenerative and distributive by design. [[Sources/doughnut-economics|Full notes]]

### Five Capitals Model (Porritt)
[The Five Capitals](https://www.forumforthefuture.org/the-five-capitals) — A framework developed by Forum for the Future that identifies five types of capital: natural, human, social, manufactured, and financial. Sustainability requires maintaining and enhancing all five, rather than converting natural and social capital into financial capital. [[Sources/five-capitals-model|Full notes]]

### TEEB — The Economics of Ecosystems and Biodiversity
[TEEB](https://www.unep.org/topics/teeb) — A UNEP global initiative that makes nature's values visible to decision-makers. Provides tools and approaches for mainstreaming biodiversity and ecosystem values into policy and planning.

## Key Claims

- GDP growth alone is an inadequate measure of progress; it ignores ecological degradation and social inequality
- Natural capital underpins all other forms of capital — its depletion undermines long-term economic viability
- Climate and biodiversity crises are coupled and require integrated economic responses
