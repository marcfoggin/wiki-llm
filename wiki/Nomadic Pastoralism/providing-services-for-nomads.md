## Overview

Conventional service delivery — schools, clinics, government offices — assumes a settled population. This creates a structural gap for mobile communities. Pastoralist children are disproportionately out of school, pastoral areas have the worst health indicators, and nomadic communities are typically underrepresented in governance.

Closing this gap requires services designed around mobility: mobile health clinics, distance and boarding education adapted to pastoral calendars, livestock-based insurance systems, and governance structures that include pastoral voices.

The challenge is not merely logistical. Sedentary service models carry implicit assumptions that settlement is progress and mobility is a problem to be solved. A genuinely inclusive approach treats mobility as a legitimate livelihood and designs services that support it.

## Key Claims

- Service provision gaps are a major driver of pastoral marginalisation
- Education and health systems designed for settled populations systematically exclude nomads
- Mobile and adaptive service delivery models exist and work, but are rarely scaled
- Including pastoral communities in governance and policy design is essential for equitable service provision
