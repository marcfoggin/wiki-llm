## The Big Idea

Creation care recognises that human wellbeing is inseparable from the health of the natural world. It challenges dominant economic and development models that treat nature primarily as a resource, instead emphasising interdependence, limits, and responsibility. This perspective is especially important in mountain and rangeland systems, where ecological fragility and human livelihoods are closely linked.

Traditional ways of life often embody long-standing relationships with land, water, and biodiversity. These systems are not static but adaptive, shaped by deep knowledge of place. Modern pressures — including market integration, policy change, and climate impacts — are disrupting these relationships. There is an urgent need to reconnect development approaches with ecological realities and local knowledge.

Frameworks such as Doughnut Economics and the Five Capitals model help reframe how value is understood, moving beyond narrow financial metrics. Global agreements and conservation tools provide structure for action, but their effectiveness depends on how well they engage with local contexts and rights.

Creation care is not only about protecting nature. It is about sustaining the relationships that make life possible, and ensuring that development pathways remain within ecological boundaries while supporting human dignity and resilience.

## Why This Matters

- Ecosystems underpin livelihoods, especially in marginal environments
- Economic models often overlook ecological limits and relational values
- Biodiversity loss directly affects food systems and resilience
- Mountains and wetlands are critical for water, climate, and biodiversity
- Protected areas require inclusive, community-based governance
