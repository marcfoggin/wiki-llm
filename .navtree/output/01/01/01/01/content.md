## Overview

Pastoral identity is not merely occupational — it is cultural, spiritual, and relational. For many pastoralist communities, the relationship with livestock, land, and movement defines who they are. Cultural practices, oral traditions, social structures, and values are all shaped by the pastoral way of life.

Settled societies have historically viewed nomads as marginal, backward, or threatening. This framing has justified policies of sedentarisation, land dispossession, and cultural erasure. Yet nomadic peoples have been central to human civilisation — catalysing trade, connecting cultures, and developing adaptive strategies that shaped history.

Recognising pastoral identity means engaging with communities on their own terms, not treating settlement as the default aspiration. Development and conservation approaches that ignore cultural identity risk undermining the very social fabric that sustains pastoral livelihoods and ecological stewardship.

## Key Claims

- Pastoral identity is cultural and relational, not merely economic — it shapes values, governance, and ecological practice
- Settled societies have systematically undervalued nomadic contributions to civilisation
- Sedentarisation policies cause cultural erosion, livelihood loss, and ecological degradation
- Effective development must engage with pastoral identity rather than seeking to replace it
