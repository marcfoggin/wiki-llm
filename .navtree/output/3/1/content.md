## Overview

Mobility is the defining feature of pastoral livelihoods. In variable environments — drylands, mountains, savannas — resources are patchy and unpredictable. Moving herds to follow water and pasture is not a sign of backwardness but an ecologically rational strategy that sustains both livestock and the landscape.

Yet mobility is increasingly constrained. Land privatisation, border closures, agricultural expansion, and sedentary development models restrict pastoral movement. When mobility is blocked, the consequences are severe: overgrazing of remaining areas, herd losses, livelihood collapse, and conflict.

Enabling pastoral mobility requires legal recognition of customary routes and access rights, cross-border agreements, and development policies designed around movement rather than settlement.

## Key Claims

- Pastoral mobility is an adaptive strategy, not a failure to settle — it enables sustainable use of variable resources
- Restricting mobility leads to ecological degradation and livelihood collapse
- Legal and policy frameworks rarely accommodate mobile livelihoods, creating systemic marginalisation
- Cross-border mobility is essential in many pastoral systems but is undermined by national borders
